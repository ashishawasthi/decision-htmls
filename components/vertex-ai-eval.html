Vertex AI Eval in agentic systems

Vertex AI Eval scores model and agent output on metrics you choose: computed metrics, model-based (LLM-as-judge) scoring, and custom rubrics, usually run on a sampled, asynchronous stream of production traffic rather than in the request path. Quality is not something a latency dashboard can see; you have to grade the answers, and grading every response with a judge model is too slow and too costly to sit inline. The design rule that follows: keep eval off the hot path, run it on a small async sample, and use it to watch faithfulness, citation accuracy, and drift and to settle champion-challenger comparisons. Reflects Google Cloud guidance as of May 2026; verify against current Google Cloud docs.

Role

The role is consistent across an interactive assistant and a task automation system: Vertex AI Eval is the output-quality measurement layer, running asynchronously beside production. What shifts is the metric set. An assistant leans on faithfulness, helpfulness, and citation accuracy; automation leans on task success and schema or format correctness. The placement, sampled and async, stays the same.

When to use, when not

Good fit

  • Measuring faithfulness and citation accuracy on RAG answers so you can catch hallucination and ungrounded claims.
  • Drift monitoring: tracking whether quality metrics slide after a model, prompt, or data change.
  • Champion-challenger and A/B comparisons between two models or two prompts on the same inputs.
  • Standardizing on managed computed and model-based metrics instead of hand-rolling and maintaining a judge harness.

Avoid

  • Synchronous, per-request gating: a judge-model call in the hot path adds seconds and cost. Sample async instead.
  • Latency or availability questions: that is Cloud Trace and Cloud Monitoring, not eval.
  • As the sole arbiter on high-stakes output: keep a human-in-the-loop review for the riskiest decisions.
  • Grading 100% of traffic with a large judge model: the token bill scales with volume; sample.

Alternatives, and how to choose

What you need Use instead Typical latency / cost
Managed eval with computed, model-based, and custom metrics on Vertex Vertex AI Eval (Gen AI evaluation service) per eval run; judge-model tokens dominate
Full control over metrics and harness, portable across clouds DIY eval harness or open frameworks (for example Ragas, DeepEval) low license cost; engineering and judge-token cost
Definitive sign-off on high-stakes or ambiguous output Human review / human-in-the-loop labeling slow and expensive per item; highest trust
Latency, errors, and availability, not quality Cloud Trace and Cloud Monitoring per span / per time series

Latency and cost profile

  • Latency: not on the user-facing path. Eval runs asynchronously on a sampled stream, so judge-model latency (often several seconds per item) never reaches the end user. Results land in dashboards and logs minutes later, which is fine for monitoring and comparison.
  • Cost: driven by eval runs, and within a run by judge-model tokens: a larger judge model and longer inputs and outputs cost more per scored item. Sampling rate is the dominant lever, which is why production eval runs on a small fraction of traffic rather than everything. Verify current rates against Google Cloud pricing.

Integration patterns and tuning

  • Sample, don't grade everything: tee a small percentage of production requests and responses to an async eval job. This holds cost down while still surfacing drift and regressions.
  • Pick metrics that match the job: faithfulness and groundedness for RAG, citation accuracy where sources are shown, plus custom rubric metrics for domain-specific quality.
  • Choose the judge deliberately: a stronger judge model grades better but costs more per item; match judge size to how high-stakes the decision is.
  • Run champion-challenger: score the current and candidate model or prompt on the same sampled inputs and compare before you promote a change.
  • Close the loop with humans: route low-scoring or flagged items to human review, and feed corrections back to improve prompts, retrieval, and the eval rubric itself.

How it maps to this tool

This component backs the Eval observability row, shown running on roughly a 5% asynchronous sample of production traffic. That number encodes the core rule: continuous quality measurement on a sampled stream, off the request path, so you watch faithfulness and drift without paying judge-model cost on every call.

It anchors the human feedback loop. The designer pairs sampled eval with human review: automated scores flag the suspect responses, humans adjudicate the hardest cases, and that signal feeds back into prompts, retrieval, and champion-challenger decisions.
